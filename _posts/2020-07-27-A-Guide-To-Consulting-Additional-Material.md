University courses or modules often come with prescribed or recommended material for students to engage with. Oftentimes instructors refer to it as “all you need” to get through and even do well in the course, 🙂 but is it ? Well, as it turns out, students sometimes consult material beyond just the “all you need” material. Even though they may or may not have valid reasons for doing so, it is still vital for one to understand the pros and cons of this and learn how to do it properly when necessary.

Common Reasons for Consulting Additional Material

1. No reason at all – yes, some students do actually just dive into additional material even though they have no idea why, whether they understand prescribed material or not, and what exactly do they want out of additional material. The common tool for these people are YouTube videos.

2. Failure to understand prescribed material – trivial one indeed yet broad. This may be due to a multitude of reasons including generally badly structured or badly presented course prescribed material, and material presented in a format incompatible with the student’s cognitive skills.

3. Gaps in prescribed material and desire for more information – additional material seem to have the potential to fill these gaps, and provide more information for those looking to expand their knowledge beyond what they are being taught.

Some Things to Consider when Consulting Additional Sources

1. Source Integrity

One of the common issues in academia is the use of untrustworthy sources (which is why the endless debate on the use of Wikipedia). It is understandable that most of the sources students use cannot be classified as scholarly sources, it is still important to at least have some confidence about the validity of the source before using it. For example, in a case of a YouTube channel, one can do some background check on the presenter or get comments from people who have used it before.

2. Time

Time is a resource, and so, it should not be wasted impulsively and aimlessly surfing the internet looking for one thing for hours. It is important for one to make sure that consulting additional material does not take too much time as it should, and does not interfere with ones course work. This can be done by avoiding super long material such as a 2+ hours YouTube video or 50 pages article on just one small concept, and also making sure every second spent on something counts, rather than spending a lot of time on something without full attention.

3. Information Overload

One should be aware of information overload. Having purpose and clear objectives before consulting a resource can save one from information overload and time wasting, as they know exactly what they are looking for, and not just going through everything that is present. This should be complemented by proper engagement with prescribed course work, after which one can identify gaps and loopholes in their knowledge and directly target these when consulting additional resources.

4. Information structure and style

Last but not least, recognizing differences in the presentation style, notation and structure of additional and prescribed resources is vital. Sometimes success in a course is not only determined by one’s knowledge but also one’s ability to present that knowledge as required by the instructor. And so, being able to translate and infuse knowledge obtained from additional sources into the course specific style is an absolute requirement to avoid endless debates on why you were marked wrong on something you had right.
